How Does Dry Ice Blasting Work?

Dry ice blasting is a non-destructive cleaning process that uses solid carbon dioxide to dislodge residue while leaving the surface intact. Dry ice blasting is similar to pressure washing. It uses compressed air to propel media at high speed towards a surface. In this case, the media are carbon dioxide particles, which form solid pellets when cooled to -109.3 degrees Fahrenheit. Blasting frozen carbon dioxide onto a surface has three immediate effects:

  • The surface's contents freeze and fracture due to thermal shock before the pellets even make contact.
  • The high-speed collision between the pellets and the brittle surface contaminants breaks unwanted residue into small pieces.
  • The dry ice quickly vaporizes when exposed to ambient air at room temperature. This results in more debris being released.

Here are a few of the benefits to dry ice blasting:

More About Dry Ice Blasting

The three ways in which dry ice blasting works on your surface cleaning project:

  • Thermal effect cleaning action. As the dry ice strikes the substrate surface, the temperature of both the dirt/residue layer and the substrate decreases. The freezing cold ice hitting the surface lowers the temperatures of different materials in unequal ways, for instance food residue freezes faster than the stainless steel surface underneath, and this effect causes the food to shrink and releases surface adhesion or surface tension, allowing the impact to remove the debris. This thermal effect helps to separate the dirt/residue from the substrate.
  • Sublimation refers to the process where dry ice is converted almost immediately back into gas. The ice-to-gas conversion occurs when the solid ice hits the surface. This releases the entire energy used to make the gas solid. It also creates an explosive reaction that multiplies the cleaning force, trapping dirt and increasing its potency.

What is Dry Ice used in Cleaning?

Dry ice cleaning and blasting uses compressed air to force CO2 pellets out of a tube through a narrow tip aimed at the surface to be cleaned. It looks similar to sandblasting or pressure washing, but the main difference is dry ice. The CO2 is so cold - 109deg below zero - that it causes the dirt molecules to shrink, loosening them from the surface. As soon as the ice pellets hit the surface they change from solid to gas, creating a molecular "blast" that pries off dirt, grime and paint without significantly lowering the temperature of the surface.

  • Only thing you need to do now is clean up any paint, glue, resins, asphalt or grime that has been left behind. There is no waste water. No chemical drainage. No sand or plastic beads scattered around. The CO2 solid pellets dissipate into the air and are absorbed by the atmosphere.
  • There is no dust or detritus from dry ice blasting that can be trapped in or foul up either the machinery being cleaned or nearby equipment.
  • They are safe for the workplace and do not pose a danger to equipment or people.

Ice Cleaning eliminates the need to disassemble industrial equipment and move it to an area for cleaning. Then, it can be returned to its original location to be reassembled. This allows you to clean it in situ, which greatly reduces downtime. Dry ice blasting is very agile and can reach most areas. You can clean the entire thing by doing some in-place disassembly if necessary.

Comparison of Dry Ice Blasting to Other Methods

There are many available methods for cleaning industrial equipment. Each method has advantages and drawbacks, and some are comparable to dry ice blasting in some areas while falling short in others. This is how other cleaning methods stack up against dry ice blasting:

  • Abrasive blasting. Unlike dry ice blasting, abrasive blasting is highly toxic and dangerous for the operator. To avoid being exposed to fumes or dust, the operator should wear a mask and a breathing device.
  • Soda blasting. Abrasive blasting can be used in a variety of ways, including soda blasting.
  • You can pressure wash. Pressure washing, like dry ice blasting is not abrasive. It does not cause any harm to the environment by emitting or toxic chemicals. This method, however, is not electrically conductive and requires wastewater disposal.
  • Tools for hand. Hand tools are environmentally safe, don't use toxic materials, require no special waste considerations, and are not electrically conductive. Hand tools have a lot of disadvantages, including abrasiveness and additional labor requirements.

There are a variety of safety benefits included with dry ice blasting which might not be found using other industrial cleaning methods. The most prominent of these include:

  • Elimination of toxic chemicals and emissions
  • Reduced waste of cleaning solutions
  • Surface degradation by abrasives prevented
